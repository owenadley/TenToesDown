Overlapping the Flavours - There's Nothing In The Annex
November, 2016
By: Lucie Pelletier



Music these days is either one of two things; revolutionary, or pretentious. Many bands get twisted and morphed into a display of unoriginality and lose their native sound. On October 21st of 2016, Toronto based transgressive and alternative indie band “Overlapse” released their EP ‘There’s Nothing In The Annex’. With evident musical influences from multiple genres, this revolutionary Chet Faker-Zayn Malik-The Weekend-Oh Wonder sounding album has created a infections buzz in the r&b scene.


What is so spectacular about the album is there’s a piece for everyone to relate to. I was thoroughly impressed with the array of musical variety in the EP. Track 7 ‘Alligator’ surprised and drew me in the most. Although the theme of the album resonated through trance like tones, this track started with a classical approach and featured a magical piano interlude. Most modern artists these days have a hard time displaying diverse musical abilities, but Overlapse made sure to defy those stereotypes with their obvious ability in multiple abstract and contrasting vibes. By creating a collage of musical awareness, Overlapse was able to debut a central theme that stayed constant in every track- the vocals. The vocal production of this EP is out of this world. I mean, maybe we’re all just too used to hearing overproduced tones and unnatural ranges, but the way the vocals were recorded creates an absolute subversive trance. Completely haunting and emotional with a faint resonance of what I would imagine would taste of dark chocolate.


While dissecting their song ‘165’ off the EP, I found multiple layers devoted to creative interpretations of genres. If you find yourself a lover of rap music they touch on that. If you find yourself needing that deep groove and drive of a beat they have that covered. If you like chill and ambient tunes to zone out or jam out to, they feature that. If you enjoy haunting female vocalists or resonating male tones, Overlapse pairs the two together like chocolate and vanilla. That is the main theme I found most prominent. With having a dark driven beat drizzled with chill techno trills then topped with these smooth, creamy vanilla vocals, There’s Nothing In The Annex creates the most experimental and satisfying sundae for musicians to eat. That spoon will be licked clean.
